TL;DR

DYG allows users to drag 3D Gaussian scene, achieving flexible and precise editing results.

Abstract

Recent advancements in 3D scene editing have been propelled by the rapid development of generative models. Existing methods typically utilize generative models to perform text-guided editing of 3D representations, such as 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS). However, these methods are often limited to texture modifications and fail when addressing large-scale geometric changes, such as editing a character's head to turn around. Moreover, such methods lack accurate control over the position of editing results, as language struggles to precisely describe the extent of edits. To this end, we introduce DYG, an effective 3D drag-based editing method for 3D Gaussian Splatting. It enables users to conveniently specify the target dragging region and the desired dragging direction through the input of 3D masks and pairs of control points, thereby achieving flexible and precise editing results. Specifically, given the discrete and non-structural nature of 3DGS, which poses challenges for edits In unevenly distributed 3DGS fields, DYG introduces Triplane to predict the position shifts of 3DGS, thereby establishing the scaffold of the editing results. The Region-Specific Positional Decoder and Smooth Local Edition modules are proposed to focus the editing on the target region while ensuring other areas of the scene remain unchanged. Additionally, our proposed score distillation loss function, Drag-SDS, enables multi-view consistent editing results. Extensive experiments demonstrate that DYG conducts effective drag-based editing following the control points prompts, surpassing other baselines in terms of editing effect and quality, both qualitatively and quantitatively.

Pipeline

The overall framework of DYG. Given a 3D Gaussian scene, the user inputs 3D Masks and several pairs of 3D control points. Top: the 3D Gaussian scene is encoded using a Multi-scale Triplane Positional Encoder to capture the position of the 3D Gaussians We apply a Region-Specific Positional Encoder to differentiate the decoding of Gaussians inside the 3D Mask from those outside, obtaining positional offsets. In this process, we apply Smooth Local Edition to smooth the deformation inside and outside the Mask to prevent boundary geometric tearing, while applying regularization loss on the Gaussians outside the Mask to encourage them to remain unchanged. Bottom: For the initial scene, user-input 3D Mask, and 3D drag points, we perform rendering and projection to obtain 2D initial images, 2D Masks, and 2D control points. These are then passed through an encoder to obtain conditional embeddings. The conditional embedding and noise-added Gaussian forward-rendered images are input into a 2D Drag-Based Latent Diffusion Model (LDM). We optimize the 3D Gaussian properties and Triplane using our proposed Score Distillation loss function, Drag-SDS, to generate multi-view consistent editing results.
